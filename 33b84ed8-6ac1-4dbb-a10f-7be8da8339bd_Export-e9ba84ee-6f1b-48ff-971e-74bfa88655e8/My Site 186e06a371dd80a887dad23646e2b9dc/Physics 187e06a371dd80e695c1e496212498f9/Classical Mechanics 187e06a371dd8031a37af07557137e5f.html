What is Classical Mechanics?

Classical Mechanics, or CM is basically the study of macro-sized objects, which falls outside the domain of quantum mechanics, and electromagnetism although one could include E&M into classical mechanics, but most just treat EM as its own thing, despite Maxwell’s eqs coming into formulation well before the “quantum” revolution, and special/general relativity.

Classical mechanics can basically be split into 3 parts,

  • Newtonian Mechanics
  • Lagrangian Mechanics
  • Hamiltonian Mechanics

Newtonian Mechanics involves using Newton’s laws, namely the 2nd law ( F=maF=maF=ma﻿ ) to solve dynamic systems. One generally studies kinematics, dynamics, energy, momentum/angular momentum, and oscillations in the newtonian part of a CM course.

The Lagrangian method involves solving classical mechanics problems in a different way, primarily using the Euler-Lagrange equations/Hamilton’s principle instead of Newton’s 2nd law, although they are equivalent. The Lagrangian method is often more helpful in more complicated problems, and is used in QM as well; using the principle of least action in QM was one of Feynman’s big contributions with path integrals.

Books/Resources

To summarize from my book rec page, I’d recommend:

Morin or Kleppner/Kolenkow for a first time introduction to physics, of course calculus is a prerequisite. These books do a good job of showing how to solve problems if you’ve never done any physics before. K&K only does Newtonian Mechanics + some special relativity (like most CM books these days.) Morin does some Lagrangian stuff, and is sorta geared towards a “smart” first year, or honors class.

Slightly more advanced but still beginner friendly is Taylor. However, he does lack depth on some topics (especially angular momentum in the Newtonian section). He is easy to read, however. But I do think the problems in K&K and Morin are much better for learning actual physics. Taylor is in general a great reference book, though, and I wouldn’t say it’s bad at all.

A book that is similar to Taylor, if for some reason you just don’t want to read Taylor, is Marion and Thornton’s Classical Dynamics.

Both of these books cover Newtonian, Lagrangian, and Hamiltonian mechanics, and Special Relativity.

What was more of a mathematical introduction, and something that I think that I liked more, even though the topic organization was really all over the place- was Kibble/Berkshire’s Classical Mechanics book. Felt really clean and as a math student I liked it. I got this recommendation from David Tong’s lecture notes, which are free and really, really good. Tong’s notes are similar in style to Kibble but more organized, thankfully.

The only “problem” with Tong’s lecture notes is that he doesn’t have as many problems as an actual book would have, but that’s not really a fault since they are lecture notes, and very well done for lecture notes; you can probably just snag extra problems from some actual textbook.

For grad level classical mechanics, there is of course, Goldstein (2nd or 3rd edition, but I’ve heard some bad things about the 3rd one). and David Tong as well. Although, in my opinion, the stuff in Taylor/Morin is enough, if you want to study anything in undergrad physics, like EM, QM, QFT, GR, etc…
