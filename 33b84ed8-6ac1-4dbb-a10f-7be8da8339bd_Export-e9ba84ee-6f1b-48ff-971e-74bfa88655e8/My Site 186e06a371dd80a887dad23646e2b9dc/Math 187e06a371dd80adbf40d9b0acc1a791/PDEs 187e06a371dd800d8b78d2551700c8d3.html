What are PDEs?

PDEs, as opposed to ODEs, are the study of partial differential equations, ie DEs where the unknown/dependent function, usually denoted by a uuu﻿ in the early stages of the topic is a function of multiple variables. For example, if u(x,t) ⁣:R2→Ru(x,t) \colon \R^2 \to \Ru(x,t):R2→R﻿ is a function of a thin rod’s temperature with insulation on the sides, then we can have a heat equation

And we can solve for a solution if we are given suitable boundary conditions.

PDEs are important because in real life, most functions in modelling phenomena are often multi-variable. When studying PDEs, most of the theory is motivated by concrete examples, as opposed to ODEs where one generally focuses on solving abstract ODEs and then looks at a few special cases for applications (like Newton’s laws, population growth, oscillations, etc.)

How to Study PDEs?

Well, you can study PDEs from the more applied POV, ie, just solving PDEs analytically, using various techniques. This can be done after a course in ODEs, and multivariable calculus.

However, the pure math side of PDEs is definitely more interesting, rigorous… and difficult. One usually needs at least Functional Analysis in the toolbelt before beginning to study PDEs rigorously, and probably a course based on solving them as well.

For a first experience with PDEs, ie just learning to solve them, I’d recommend Farlow’s book, and then after learning some measure theory + functional analysis, either Evans’ book (which is quite popular). However, I’m planning to study diff geo first, and then dive into Taylor’s 3 volume set, which is sort of regarded as a bible for PDEs, and is a lot more thorough and advanced than Evans.
